19	## 2. What 4M expects from a new modality — the binding constraint
20	
21	Before designing anything, the codebook size we ship has to fit 4M's modality
22	registration model. Read [`../../external/ml-4m/fourm/data/modality_info.py`](../../external/ml-4m/fourm/data/modality_info.py)
23	— that file is the source of truth.
24	
25	Concrete facts that drive the design:
26	
27	- **No fixed vocab across modalities.** Each modality declares its own
28	  `vocab_size` in `MODALITY_INFO`. Image-token modalities range from 4096
29	  (semseg) to 16384 (RGB); text/sequence is 30_000; `human_poses` "borrows"
30	  the 30k text vocab via `shared_vocab: ['caption']`. We are free to pick.
31	- **But vocab IS fixed at registration time.** 4M allocates a learnable
32	  embedding `[vocab_size, d_model]` for the modality. Adding tokens
33	  mid-training breaks that table. **Pick V before computing the token cache
34	  that 4M trains on; commit to it.**
35	- **`type: 'seq'` is the right modality type for MEG**, not image-grid.
36	  Mirror `human_poses` (`modality_info.py:172`) more than `tok_rgb`. The
37	  per-channel-per-timestep token stream is a sequence, not a 2D grid.
38	  Pose-tokenizer registration template:
39	  `vocab_size`, `encoder_embedding = SequenceEncoderEmbedding(...)`,
40	  `decoder_embedding = SequenceDecoderEmbedding(...)`,
41	  `max_length`, `num_channels`, `type: 'seq'`,
42	  `id: generate_uint15_hash('meg_tokens')`.
43	- **Multi-codebook (`num_codebooks > 1`) is supported by the `memcodes`
44	  quantizer** (used by `human_poses`, 1024 × 8). Cho 2026's tokenizers use
45	  a single codebook per channel. Stick with **`num_codebooks = 1`** for MEG
46	  — there is no reason to fight the upstream tokenizer's design.
47	- **Whether to share vocab with another modality is a 4M-side decision.**
48	  Human_poses reuses the text vocab because pose tokens are mapped into the
49	  WordPiece range; this lets a 4M decoder generate poses with the same head
50	  as captions. We do **not** want that for MEG — it couples MEG to text
51	  training. **Register a dedicated `meg_tokens` modality with its own
52	  vocab.**
53	
54	So the headline constraint: pick `V` and `max_length` (= per-trial token
55	sequence length) when you write the tokenizer, because the modality
56	registration that goes into 4M has to declare both.

162	## 5. What "recalibration" / "finetuning" actually mean here
163	
164	The Cho 2026 paper warns that both tokenizer families need dataset-specific
165	adaptation. The mechanism is different for each.
166	
167	### Phase 1 — μ-transform recalibration (no gradient descent, but still a `fit` step)
168	
169	The μ-transform pipeline (paper §3.3.1):
170	
171	1. **Clip** the input to a percentile range `[q_lo, q_hi]` to suppress
172	   outliers.
173	2. **Max-abs scale** to `[−1, 1]`: `x' = x / s` with `s = max|x|`.
174	3. **μ-law compand**: `F(x') = sgn(x') · ln(1 + μ|x'|) / ln(1 + μ)`.
175	4. **Uniform bin** the compressed values into `V` bins → integer token IDs.
176	
177	The reference config Cho 2026 ships
178	([`../../external/Cho2026_Tokenizer/models/tokenizer/mu_transform/config.yml`](../../external/Cho2026_Tokenizer/models/tokenizer/mu_transform/config.yml))
179	is:
180	
181	```yaml
182	mu: 255
183	n_tokens: 256
184	normalization: max_abs
185	```
186	
187	There are **no learned weights**, but two parameters are dataset-dependent
188	and must be fit on a THINGS-MEG training split, frozen, and reused at
189	inference:
190	
191	- `s = max|x|`: the per-channel (recommended) or global max-abs scaler.
192	- `q_lo, q_hi`: clip thresholds (defaults around the 0.5 / 99.5 percentiles).
193	
194	`μ` and `V` are hyperparameters, not fit from data — start with μ=255,
195	V=256.
196	
197	Concretely: there is no `.train()` loop. There is a `fit(train_dataset) →
198	calibration.json` step that produces a small JSON sidecar with
199	`{s_per_channel, q_lo, q_hi, mu, V, channel_mode}`. At inference we load
200	that JSON and apply the stateless transform. Treat the calibration JSON as
201	a versioned tokenizer checkpoint.
202	
203	**Phase 1 status (shipped):** calibrated on THINGS-MEG train split with
204	μ=255, V=256, per-channel clip `[0.5, 99.5]` percentiles. Run slug:
205	`V256_mu255_clip0.5-99.5_per_channel_s0`. Eval via
206	`modal_meg_eval.py --tokenizer mu_transform` (see §9).
207	
208	> Verified §3 fact that matters here: all 271 channels are `mag`, so the
209	> grad-vs-mag amplitude disparity problem does not apply. Per-channel `s`
210	> is still the safer default (inter-sensor amplitude variance is real even
211	> within one channel type), but the bin-saturation risk is much lower than
212	> it would be on a mixed-sensor dataset.

214	### Phase 2 — Cho 2026 learnable tokenizer (actual gradient training)
215	
216	The Cho 2026 learnable tokenizer (paper §3.2) is an **autoencoder**, not a
217	VQ-VAE: encoder is GRU → Dense → LayerNorm producing per-time-step logits
218	over a vocab of size `V`; decoder is a bank of `V` 1-D conv kernels (causal
219	or non-causal) summed with learned per-token gains. Trained end-to-end with
220	MSE + an annealed soft-to-hard argmax relaxation, then unused tokens are
221	pruned ("token refactorization").
222	
223	Reference configs they ship
224	(`../../external/Cho2026_Tokenizer/models/tokenizer/{causal,noncausal}/config.yml`):
225	
226	```yaml
227	sequence_length: 200
228	n_channels: 52         # NB: 52 source-parcellated channels, NOT sensor
229	n_tokens: 128
230	token_dim: 10
231	rnn_n_units: 128
232	token_kernel_padding: causal  # or `same` for non-causal
233	training: { lr: 1e-5, batch: 32, epochs: 40, anneal_stages: 40 }
234	```
235	
236	Three things the configs tell us, in light of the verified data spec:
237	
238	1. **They trained on source-reconstructed data (52 parcels), not sensor
239	   space.** Our THINGS-MEG is **271-sensor**. The tokenizer is applied
240	   per-channel and the GRU has no channel-conditioning, so weights *can*
241	   be reused on sensor data, but the *amplitude distribution* of source
242	   parcels (after `osl_dynamics.standardize()`) is not identical to sensor
243	   amplitudes. This is the symmetric MEG version of the LABRAM/THINGS
244	   distribution mismatch in the parent doc.
245	2. **Their sequence_length = 200 samples ≈ 0.8 s.** Our trials are 281
246	   samples (1.4 s). Either reduce our windows to 200 samples (matches their
247	   training distribution → reuse pretrained weights cleanly) or retrain
248	   with `sequence_length = 281`. Strong prior: **window to 200** for 2a/2b
249	   to keep the pretrained encoder in-distribution; only change
250	   `sequence_length` for 2c.
251	3. **Their LR (1e-5) is very small.** Tells us finetuning is intended to be
252	   conservative — they expect the loss landscape to be flat around the
253	   pretrained optimum. Start with that LR and only increase if the loss is
254	   not moving after 1 epoch.
255	
256	"Finetuning on our dataset" then has three concrete recipes:
257	
258	- **2a.** Load pretrained weights, apply on THINGS-MEG **as-is** (zero
259	  finetune). Establishes the floor for what their pretrained tokenizer
260	  buys us on distribution-shifted data.
261	- **2b.** Load pretrained weights, continue MSE training on THINGS-MEG
262	  (lr=1e-5, anneal κ already at 0 → hard tokens — or restart anneal).
263	  This is the "actual finetune."
264	- **2c.** Take their architecture, train from scratch on THINGS-MEG. This
265	  is needed if (a) pretrained weights fail the §5 harness even after 2b,
266	  (b) we want to change `sequence_length` or `n_tokens`, or (c) the
267	  source-vs-sensor mismatch turns out to dominate. With ~108k trials
268	  pooled across P1–P4 (see §3 — much more than the parent doc estimated),
269	  from-scratch is more viable than first assumed.
270	
271	Run **2a → 2b → 2c** in order; stop as soon as the harness numbers are
272	acceptable. Do not skip 2a — the gap between 2a and 2b is the answer to
273	"how much did pretraining buy us."
274	
275	> System-level note: their pretraining data is **resting-state** MEG;
276	> THINGS-MEG is **event-related** (stimulus-locked). Expect the pretrained
277	> kernels to capture continuous oscillatory structure well and to
278	> underweight evoked transients. Reconstruction MSE may look fine while
279	> the §5.3 linear probe quietly tanks — watch that metric closely after
280	> each of 2a/2b/2c.

467	## 8. Evaluation contract
468	
469	Identical to the parent §5. The same `test_tokenizer.py` harness must pass
470	for each of the three MEG tokenizers before it is wired into 4M:
471	
472	1. Reconstruction fidelity (time MSE + per-channel Pearson + **spectral
473	   MSE**). Spectral MSE is the diagnostic metric for MEG — band power
474	   (especially α/β) is what carries task signal. PSD range: 1–100 Hz
475	   (Nyquist for 200 Hz data is 100 Hz).
476	2. Codebook utilization (perplexity, dead-code fraction). The μ-transform's
477	   dead-code fraction is the metric to watch; Cho 2026 prunes dead codes
478	   ("token refactorization") so its effective `V*` can be reported
479	   alongside nominal `V`.
480	3. Linear probe on THINGS labels. With 22,449 image-level codes / 1854
481	   concepts / 27 superordinate categories, **the harness uses 27-way
482	   superordinate as the sole probe target.** The 1854-way concept space
483	   was tried first and sat at chance — at ~3k eval trials it gives
484	   ~2.6 trials/class, too few for a linear head to fit. The
485	   `image_id → concept_id` mapping comes from THINGS-MEG OpenNeuro
486	   events.tsv (see `modal_download_things_labels.py`); the
487	   `concept_id → superordinate_27` mapping comes from canonical THINGS
488	   metadata (`category_mat_manual.tsv` in ViCCo-Group/THINGS-data) via
489	   `modal_download_things_superordinate.py`. Both files are checked into
490	   git under `meg/data/`. The probe uses **5-fold CV** and reports
491	   `top{k}_<set>_mean` / `_std` across folds. For BrainOmni it
492	   additionally reports an RVQ0-only feature set (`tokens_rvq0_*`) next
493	   to the full-RVQ-sum (`tokens_all_*`) — see
494	   `notes/linear_probe_design.md` for the rationale.
495	4. Token-sequence statistics (unigram entropy, bigram conditional entropy,
496	   run-length). The μ-transform is at particular risk of degenerate run
497	   lengths on slow drifts — watch this.
498	
499	MEG-specific harness parameters (PSD frequency range, named bands) go in
500	`meg_config.py` and are passed into the harness as `EvalConfig`. Do not
501	hardcode them.
502

521	## 9. §5 leaderboard — μ-transform vs BrainOmni (2026-05-22)
522	
523	All runs: **n=3000 test trials**, **seed=0**, learnable split 80/10/10
524	(BrainOmni) / μ-transform uses same test indices via `splits.py`. Eval JSONs
525	under each run's `evals/` folder.
526	
527	### Reconstruction (§5.1)
528	
529	| Metric | μ-transform | BrainOmni 3a (zero-shot) | BrainOmni 3b (finetuned) |
530	|--------|-------------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
531	| MSE ↓ | **0.999** | 4.35 | 1.20 |
532	| Channel Pearson ↑ | **0.998** | 0.345 | 0.870 |
533	| PSD MSE ↓ | 1.01×10⁹ | 1.88×10⁸ | **6.13×10⁷** |
534	
535	μ-transform's near-perfect channel correlation is **misleading** — it is a
536	near-invertible per-sample codec (clip → μ-law → 256 bins). BrainOmni is a
537	real compressor (271×281 → 512 codes). **3b finetune** closes most of the
538	reconstruction gap vs 3a; μ-transform still wins trivial round-trip fidelity.
539	
540	### Codebook (§5.2)
541	
542	| Metric | μ-transform (V=256) | BrainOmni 3a/3b (V=512) |
543	|--------|----------------------|-------------------------|
544	| Codes used | 256/256 | 512/512 |
545	| Utilization | 100% | 100% |
546	| Perplexity | 194 | 443 → **494** (3b) |
547	| Entropy (nats) | 5.27 | 6.09 → **6.20** (3b) |
548	
549	Both use full vocab. BrainOmni spreads mass over a larger codebook.
550	
551	### Sequence structure (§5.4)
552	
553	| Metric | μ-transform | BrainOmni 3a | BrainOmni 3b |
554	|--------|-------------|--------------|--------------|
555	| Entropy gap % ↑ | **22.9%** | 9.7% | 5.6% |
556	| Mean run length | 1.06 | ~1.00 | ~1.00 |
557	| frac runs ≥ 2 | **1.0** | 0.58 | 0.63 |
558	
559	Different token geometries (76k sample tokens vs 512 latent codes) — entropy
560	gap is not directly comparable. μ-transform shows strong local temporal
561	redundancy; BrainOmni tokens look more i.i.d.
562	
563	### Linear probe (§5.3) — **gate metric**
564	
565	> **Label space change (2026-05):** the probe now uses the THINGS **27
566	> high-level superordinate categories** (from `concepts-metadata_things.tsv`)
567	> instead of the 1854-way concept space — the old setup gave ~2.6 trials/class
568	> and sat at chance. Coverage drops to ~50% of trials (892 of 1854 concepts
569	> have a clean single-category Bottom-up label per Hebart 2019), but the
570	> remaining ~5,000 trials give ~185/class which the linear head can actually
571	> fit. See `notes/linear_probe_design.md` and the §5.3 docstring in
572	> `evaluation/probe.py` for the design rationale.
573	
574	**BrainOmni 3b vs raw, full ~10k test split (5032 trials with valid labels),
575	5-fold CV, class-weighted, balanced accuracy (chance = 1/27 = 3.70%):**
576	
577	| Probe variant            | tokens_all       | tokens_rvq0      | raw              | random           |
578	|--------------------------|------------------|------------------|------------------|------------------|
579	| Linear, unweighted CE    | 3.76% ± 0.07     | 3.71% ± 0.01     | **6.14% ± 0.44** | 3.70% ± 0.00     |
580	| Linear, class-weighted   | 3.35% ± 0.91     | 3.42% ± 0.38     | **5.16% ± 0.21** | 3.64% ± 0.73     |
581	| 2-layer MLP, weighted    | 3.18% ± 0.97     | 2.94% ± 0.78     | **4.31% ± 0.49** | 3.09% ± 0.82     |
582	
583	**μ-transform (V=256, BoC features, linear class-weighted, same protocol):**
584	
585	| Metric                      | tokens           | raw              | random           |
586	|-----------------------------|------------------|------------------|------------------|
587	| bal_acc (linear, weighted)  | 3.57% ± 0.47     | **5.16% ± 0.21** | 4.22% ± 0.78     |
588	
589	μ-transform passes §5.3 by the same margin BrainOmni does — i.e. it
590	doesn't. Tokens at chance, raw lifts to 5.16% (identical to BrainOmni's
591	raw because the underlying signal and probe are identical — confirms the
592	raw number is reproducible).
593	
594	Three diagnostic variants, one consistent verdict:
595	
596	- **Raw MEG carries signal**: ~5–7σ above chance in every config — there is
597	  linearly-decodable category info in the preprocessed waveform.
598	- **BrainOmni 3b tokens are at chance** in every config. Linear or MLP,
599	  unweighted or class-weighted — the tokens have nothing the probe can find.
600	  Slightly *below* chance under MLP indicates the head is fitting training
601	  noise, which only happens when no signal is present to anchor it.
602	- **`tokens_rvq0 ≈ tokens_all`** — the residual RVQ layers carry no extra
603	  task signal; the coarse layer alone is just as uninformative.
604	
605	The MLP rules out "info is there but nonlinear" — a stronger classifier
606	finds nothing either. The class-weighted runs rule out "training collapsed
607	to majority class" — `top1_random` correctly drops from 12.40% (unweighted
608	majority-floor) to 3.74% (uniform-random) when weights kick in, and tokens
609	still don't move.
610	
611	**Verdict (2026-05): BrainOmni 3b passes §5.1 (reconstruction) and §5.2
612	(codebook utilization) but FAILS §5.3. The finetune improved reconstruction
613	fidelity without preserving task-relevant signal — exactly the failure mode
614	§5.3 was designed to catch.**
615	
616	### Verdict for 4M
617	
618	| Criterion | μ-transform | BrainOmni 3b |
619	|-----------|-------------|--------------|
620	| Tokens / trial | ~76,151 | **512** |
621	| 4M context cost | Very high | **Practical** |
622	| Reconstruction | Excellent (near-identity) | Good (post-finetune) |
623	| Linear object info in tokens | None detected | None detected |
624	| Trained on THINGS | Calibration only | **Yes (adapt finetune)** |
625	
626	**Updated recommendation (2026-05):** the previous decision to ship 3b
627	*despite* probe failure rested on "probe noise at chance is uninformative."
628	With the 27-way + 5-fold + class-weighted + MLP triangulation, the probe is
629	no longer noise: it concretely shows raw MEG has decodable info that 3b's
630	tokens do not. Reconstruction-only training is not enough on this data.
631	
632	Next candidates, in order of cost:
633	
634	1. **Cheap diagnostic — image-averaged probe.** Average the 88,992 existing
635	   3b tokens by image_id (4× SNR for exp, 48× for test images) and re-run
636	   the probe. If averaged tokens lift above chance, single-trial noise was
637	   the bottleneck and step 2 is worth doing. If not, BrainOmni's training
638	   objective is the bottleneck regardless of input quality → skip to step 3.
639	2. **BrainOmni finetune on image-averaged signal.** Same `adapt` recipe but
640	   trained on averaged trials (fewer samples, higher SNR per sample).
641	3. **Move to Stage 2 (Cho2026 / EphysTokenizer).** The reconstruction
642	   objective is the root cause; another reconstruction-only model
643	   architecture is unlikely to rescue this.

115	## CNN probe head — design
116	
117	The flat MLP probe has no inductive bias for the structure that THINGS-MEG decoding actually exploits (Cichy / Hebart papers: channel-topographic patterns over time). A 2-layer MLP on flattened tokens may simply lack the right shape; per Dixen 2024 even on EEG, EEGNet/Conformer-style temporal convolutions massively outperform flat heads at this SNR.
118	
119	The new `cnn` head exploits the token tensor's natural structure rather than flattening it:
120	
121	- **For BrainOmni tokens** of shape `(B, 16 latents, 8 time, D=512 embed)`: a small **2-D CNN** with conv over `(latent × time)` and `D` as channels. Two conv blocks with batch-norm + ReLU + global average pooling → linear classifier. ~150k params.
122	- **For raw MEG** of shape `(B, 271 channels, 281 time)`: a small **1-D CNN** over time, channels as input dims. Two strided conv blocks → global average pooling → linear. ~50k params.
123	- **For random tokens**: same shape transform as `tokens` (random codes → `tokens_to_embedding` → CNN). Tests "what does the CNN do with uninformative tokens of the right shape?".
124	- **For μ-transform-style position-preserving tokens** `(B, 1, channels × time)`: reshape to `(B, channels, time)` then use the 1-D CNN — equivalent to applying the raw-MEG CNN to the dequantized signal.
125	
126	Rationale: small enough to not overfit on ~4k training trials per fold, while giving the probe the same inductive bias (temporal smoothness + latent-position locality) that real MEG decoders use.

190	## Experiment 1.5 — BrainOmni 3b_nonavg, image-averaged INPUT (diagnostic, no retrain)
191	
192	The cheap diagnostic from `meg/CLAUDE.md` §9: take the **existing 3b_nonavg** checkpoint and feed it **image-averaged trials at eval time**. No retraining — just a different input distribution at inference. Answers: is per-trial noise the bottleneck for category, or did the reconstruction objective itself drop the signal?
193	
194	Eval pipeline: full ~10k test split (10,036 trials) → average by `image_id` across subjects + within-subject reps → 2,245 unique averaged signals → tokenizer forward → 5-fold CV probe. Codebook stats unchanged (same 512-code vocab, perplexity ~493).
195	
196	> **A note on reconstruction MSE under averaging.** Time-domain MSE drops 1.07 → 0.29, but this is **not** a 3.6× improvement in the tokenizer's reconstruction quality — it's mostly the **target's noise floor falling**. BrainOmni's preprocess per-trial-per-channel z-scores both single and averaged inputs to unit variance, but the *composition* of that variance differs: in a single z-scored trial ~90% of variance is per-trial noise; in an N-rep average that fraction shrinks by 1/N before z-scoring. The bottleneck drops noise either way; with less noise in the target there's less for the tokenizer to "miss." The honest cross-regime metric is **per-channel Pearson r**, which barely moves: 0.870 (Exp 1) → 0.859 (Exp 1.5). So the right reading is "averaging gives the *probe* a cleaner target," not "the tokenizer reconstructs better" — and the probe results below confirm that the *tokens* see none of that benefit. (General rule: MSE is comparable across checkpoints under fixed input regime, NOT across input regimes or across tokenizer families.)

## What the two experiments together say

- **Reconstruction objective is the bottleneck**, not single-trial noise. Both averaged-input diagnostics and the averaged-trained Exp 2 fail to recover cat27.
- **Variance budget goes to subject/sensor features**, not to fine category structure.
- **Next intervention**: contrastive/supervised auxiliary loss, OR Stage 2 (Cho2026 / EphysTokenizer). Don't iterate further on reconstruction-only finetuning.
