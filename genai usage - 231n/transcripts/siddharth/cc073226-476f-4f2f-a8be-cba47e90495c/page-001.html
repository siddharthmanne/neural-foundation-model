29	## 2. System-level framing — why VQ-VAE per modality
30	
31	4M's transformer only sees integer token sequences. To add a modality you must
32	provide three things:
33	
34	- **Encoder** `x -> z` : continuous signal → continuous latents `(L, D)`
35	- **Quantizer** `z -> t` : continuous latents → discrete token IDs in `[0, V)`
36	- **Decoder** `t -> x_hat` : tokens → reconstructed signal (used for eval and
37	  detokenization at inference; not used during 4M training itself)
38	
39	Why this is the right abstraction:
40	- A single masked-token objective works across every modality if every modality
41	  speaks the same `(seq_len, vocab_size)` interface.
42	- Tokens are pre-computed once and cached → 4M training does not pay the
43	  tokenizer's forward cost per step.
44	- The tokenizer can be swapped without touching 4M, so we can A/B different
45	  EEG tokenizers (scratch vs LABRAM, etc.) cleanly.
46	
47	The 4M repo lives at [`../external/ml-4m/`](../external/ml-4m/). The relevant
48	entry points are:
49	
50	- [`external/ml-4m/fourm/vq/vqvae.py`](../external/ml-4m/fourm/vq/vqvae.py) — `VQVAE` class
51	- [`external/ml-4m/fourm/vq/models/`](../external/ml-4m/fourm/vq/models/) — encoder/decoder architectures
52	- [`external/ml-4m/fourm/vq/quantizers/`](../external/ml-4m/fourm/vq/quantizers/) — quantizer implementations (vanilla, `memcodes`, FSQ, …)

81	## 4. Architecture — baseline vs target
82	
83	This subdir trains tokenizers in **two explicit stages**. Stage 1 is a
84	deliberately simple baseline that we expect to be beaten; Stage 2 is the
85	actual production tokenizer that ships into 4M. Do not skip Stage 1 — the §5
86	harness only produces interpretable numbers if there is a baseline to
87	compare against.
88	
89	### Stage 1 — Baseline (start here, all modalities)
90	
91	- Backbone: `BottleneckMLP/B_6-Wi_1024` (encoder and decoder), inputs flattened
92	  to `(channels * time,)`. Simplest faithful backbone for a structured signal,
93	  mirrors 4M's existing non-image tokenizer (human poses).
94	- Loss: `smooth_l1` reconstruction in the time domain only. Single-term, easy
95	  to debug.
96	- Quantizer: `memcodes`, `codebook_size=1024`, `num_codebooks=8`,
97	  `latent_dim=1024`.
98	
99	This baseline is **expected to fail the spectral-MSE metric** in §5.1 — that
100	failure is the motivation for Stage 2.  Get clean numbers across all four §5
101	axes for this baseline before touching anything.
102	
103	### Stage 2 — Target (what actually ships into 4M)
104	
105	Whatever the final tokenizer is, two upgrades over the baseline are
106	non-negotiable:
107	
108	- **A stronger temporal-structure backbone than a flat MLP.** Neural responses
109	  are locally autocorrelated; an MLP on flattened `(C·T,)` ignores that prior.
110	  Either a 1-D CNN over the time axis or a temporal-patch transformer is the
111	  right shape, and either generalizes naturally to MEG / TVSD where `T` varies.
112	- **A spectral / band-power loss term**, added to `smooth_l1`. EEG/MEG task
113	  signal lives in frequency bands (θ/α/β/γ). Pure time-domain L1 can drive
114	  MSE down while still scrambling band power. Likely forms:
115	  - PSD MSE: `||PSD(x) - PSD(x_hat)||²` (Welch estimator)
116	  - Band-power MSE: integrate PSD over named bands and match per band
117	  - Multi-resolution STFT loss (borrowed from audio neural codecs)
118	  - Composite: `L = L1_time + λ_spec · L_spec + λ_band · L_band`, with `λ`
119	    chosen so each term contributes comparable gradient magnitude at init.
120	
121	These two requirements are about *what the tokenizer must learn*, not about
122	which codebase produces it. Two independent decisions then determine the
123	actual Stage 2 we ship — architecture (whose design) and weights (whose data):
124	
125	|                            | **Our own scratch design**       | **LABRAM's architecture**            |
126	|----------------------------|----------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
127	| **Trained from scratch**   | (2a) Generic, modality-agnostic  | (2b) Borrow design, not data         |
128	| **Pretrained + finetuned** | —                                | (2c) Borrow design AND data          |
129	
130	- **(2a) Scratch design, scratch weights.** We design the upgraded backbone
131	  (e.g. 1-D ResNet/U-Net over time + spectral loss) ourselves. Same recipe
132	  works for EEG, MEG, and TVSD. Full control; no external dependency. Risk:
133	  we may rediscover what LABRAM already figured out about EEG.
134	- **(2b) LABRAM architecture, scratch weights.** Adopt LABRAM's
135	  temporal-patch transformer + channel embeddings + multi-objective loss
136	  *as a design template*, but train weights from scratch on THINGS data.
137	  We get the structural priors (patch-wise tokenization, channel-aware
138	  attention, frequency-domain loss already in the recipe) without inheriting
139	  any potential distribution mismatch from LABRAM's pretraining corpus
140	  (mostly resting-state EEG; THINGS is event-related). Cost: more complex
141	  than 2a, and we still have only ~160k trials — the data-efficiency argument
142	  for LABRAM disappears here.
143	- **(2c) LABRAM architecture, LABRAM weights, finetuned.** Full transfer:
144	  load LABRAM's pretrained tokenizer and finetune on THINGS-EEG. This is
145	  the only path that exploits the 2,500+ hrs of EEG LABRAM saw in
146	  pretraining. EEG-only — MEG and TVSD still need 2a or 2b.
147	
148	Stage 2 selection is the open decision in §6. The §5 harness numbers — not
149	authorial preference — decide which one wins.
150	
151	- Per modality: train one tokenizer at a time. Do *not* try to share encoders
152	  across EEG and MEG until both single-modality baselines are working.

154	## 5. Evaluation contract — what `test_tokenizer.py` must enforce
155	
156	A tokenizer is a lossy compressor. `test_tokenizer.py` is the gate that any
157	tokenizer (scratch VQ-VAE, finetuned LABRAM, future TVSD tokenizer) must pass.
158	
159	The harness consumes any object implementing this interface:
160	
161	```python
162	class Tokenizer(Protocol):
163	    codebook_size: int
164	
165	    def tokenize(self, x: Tensor) -> LongTensor: ...       # (B, C, T) -> (B, ...)
166	    def decode_tokens(self, tokens: LongTensor) -> Tensor: ...  # (B, ...) -> (B, C, T)
167	    # optional: tokens_to_embedding(tokens) -> (B, L, D) for probe features
168	```
169	
170	Tokens may have any shape `(B, ...)`; the harness flattens to `(B, L)` where
171	needed. See [`evaluation/protocol.py`](evaluation/protocol.py).
172	
173	Four required evaluation axes — implement one module per axis so they can be
174	run independently:
175	
176	1. **Reconstruction fidelity** — encode → decode round trip.
177	   - Per-sample MSE and per-channel Pearson r between `x` and `x_hat`.
178	   - **Spectral MSE**: PSD of `x` vs `x_hat` (Welch). Neural data lives in
179	     bands; if alpha/beta power is wrong the tokenizer is broken even when
180	     time-domain MSE looks fine. This is the most diagnostic single metric.
181	2. **Codebook utilization**.
182	   - Perplexity `exp(H(p))` where `p` is the empirical token distribution.
183	     For `codebook_size=V`, perplexity ≪ V means dead codes.
184	   - Dead-code fraction (codes never selected on the eval set).
185	3. **Downstream linear probe** — does the token sequence still carry the
186	   information we actually care about?
187	   - *The failure mode this catches:* a tokenizer can have low reconstruction
188	     MSE (passes §5.1) and good codebook utilization (passes §5.2) while
189	     having silently thrown away the task-relevant signal. Example: the
190	     tokenizer faithfully reconstructs slow baseline drift (high-variance,
191	     drives MSE down) but loses the small evoked response that actually
192	     distinguishes one stimulus from another. Reconstruction looks great;
193	     the tokens are useless to 4M.
194	   - *The test:* freeze the trained tokenizer. Encode every THINGS trial to
195	     tokens. Train a **logistic regression** (linear probe) on those tokens
196	     to predict the trial's stimulus concept (THINGS has ~1,854 labels — use
197	     a coarser grouping or top-K if 1,854-way is too noisy). Report
198	     top-1 / top-5 accuracy on a held-out split.
199	   - *Why a linear probe and not a strong classifier:* if a linear model can
200	     read the stimulus off the tokens, the information is **linearly
201	     decodable** — which is what 4M's transformer needs from its input
202	     embeddings. A strong nonlinear classifier could rescue tokens that are
203	     entangled in ways 4M can't undo cheaply, so it would overstate
204	     tokenizer quality.
205	   - *Token representation for the probe:* either one-hot pool-then-flatten
206	     (count of each code per trial; bag-of-tokens), or embed each token via
207	     the VQ-VAE's codebook lookup and mean-pool over the sequence. Try
208	     both — they measure subtly different things (presence vs. average
209	     content).
210	   - *Reference points to bracket the score:* run the same probe on
211	     **(upper)** the raw flattened signal `x` itself (ceiling — all info is
212	     there) and on **(lower)** uniformly random tokens of the same shape
213	     (floor — no info). A good tokenizer should land closer to the upper
214	     bound than to the lower; if it sits near the floor, the tokens are not
215	     informative no matter how pretty the reconstruction looks.
216	
217	4. **Token-sequence statistics** — is the token *sequence* learnable by a
218	   transformer, or did the tokenizer collapse to a degenerate code?
219	   - *The failure mode this catches:* a tokenizer can pass §5.1, §5.2, and
220	     §5.3 while emitting sequences so predictable that 4M's masked-token
221	     objective learns nothing. Example: suppose the tokenizer outputs an
222	     8-token sequence per trial, but for any given trial it emits *the same
223	     code 8 times* (e.g. `[42, 42, 42, ..., 42]`), with the specific code
224	     varying by stimulus class. The decoder can still reconstruct (mapping
225	     code → trial), the codebook is fully used (§5.2 sees 1,024 distinct
226	     trial-level codes), and the probe works perfectly (one code = one
227	     stimulus). But 4M is trained to predict masked tokens given context —
228	     if `t₂` is always equal to `t₁`, the prediction task is trivial and the
229	     model learns nothing transferable.
230	   - *What to compute:*
231	     - **Unigram entropy** `H(t) = -Σ p(t) log p(t)` — the entropy of the
232	       marginal token distribution. Already implied by perplexity in §5.2,
233	       but report it explicitly per-position too (some positions may collapse
234	       while others don't).
235	     - **Bigram conditional entropy** `H(tᵢ | tᵢ₋₁)`. This is the key number.
236	       If a transformer's next-token prediction has nothing left to learn
237	       after seeing one neighbor, this collapses toward 0. **Healthy
238	       tokenizers have `H(tᵢ | tᵢ₋₁) ≈ H(t)` — i.e. transitions are nearly
239	       independent of the previous token.** A large gap `H(t) - H(tᵢ | tᵢ₋₁)`
240	       means the sequence is highly redundant.
241	     - **Run-length distribution** — how long are runs of the same code?
242	       Long runs are the most visually obvious form of collapse. Report mean
243	       run length and the fraction of trials with run length ≥ 2.
244	   - *Pass criterion:* bigram-vs-unigram entropy gap small (say <20%), no
245	     position with degenerate marginal, mean run length near 1. If any of
246	     these fail, the tokenizer will technically train 4M to convergence but
247	     the resulting model will not generalize — fix the tokenizer first.
248	
249	The harness should report all four for each tokenizer in a single table so we
250	can compare tokenizers side by side. Treat this as the source of truth for
251	"is tokenizer X better than tokenizer Y" — not training-loss curves.

19	## 2. What 4M expects from a new modality — the binding constraint
20	
21	Before designing anything, the codebook size we ship has to fit 4M's modality
22	registration model. Read [`../../external/ml-4m/fourm/data/modality_info.py`](../../external/ml-4m/fourm/data/modality_info.py)
23	— that file is the source of truth.
24	
25	Concrete facts that drive the design:
26	
27	- **No fixed vocab across modalities.** Each modality declares its own
28	  `vocab_size` in `MODALITY_INFO`. Image-token modalities range from 4096
29	  (semseg) to 16384 (RGB); text/sequence is 30_000; `human_poses` "borrows"
30	  the 30k text vocab via `shared_vocab: ['caption']`. We are free to pick.
31	- **But vocab IS fixed at registration time.** 4M allocates a learnable
32	  embedding `[vocab_size, d_model]` for the modality. Adding tokens
33	  mid-training breaks that table. **Pick V before computing the token cache
34	  that 4M trains on; commit to it.**
35	- **`type: 'seq'` is the right modality type for MEG**, not image-grid.
36	  Mirror `human_poses` (`modality_info.py:172`) more than `tok_rgb`. The
37	  per-channel-per-timestep token stream is a sequence, not a 2D grid.
38	  Pose-tokenizer registration template:
39	  `vocab_size`, `encoder_embedding = SequenceEncoderEmbedding(...)`,
40	  `decoder_embedding = SequenceDecoderEmbedding(...)`,
41	  `max_length`, `num_channels`, `type: 'seq'`,
42	  `id: generate_uint15_hash('meg_tokens')`.
43	- **Multi-codebook (`num_codebooks > 1`) is supported by the `memcodes`
44	  quantizer** (used by `human_poses`, 1024 × 8). Cho 2026's tokenizers use
45	  a single codebook per channel. Stick with **`num_codebooks = 1`** for MEG
46	  — there is no reason to fight the upstream tokenizer's design.
47	- **Whether to share vocab with another modality is a 4M-side decision.**
48	  Human_poses reuses the text vocab because pose tokens are mapped into the
49	  WordPiece range; this lets a 4M decoder generate poses with the same head
50	  as captions. We do **not** want that for MEG — it couples MEG to text
51	  training. **Register a dedicated `meg_tokens` modality with its own
52	  vocab.**
53	
54	So the headline constraint: pick `V` and `max_length` (= per-trial token
55	sequence length) when you write the tokenizer, because the modality
56	registration that goes into 4M has to declare both.

162	## 5. What "recalibration" / "finetuning" actually mean here
163	
164	The Cho 2026 paper warns that both tokenizer families need dataset-specific
165	adaptation. The mechanism is different for each.
166	
167	### Phase 1 — μ-transform recalibration (no gradient descent, but still a `fit` step)
168	
169	The μ-transform pipeline (paper §3.3.1):
170	
171	1. **Clip** the input to a percentile range `[q_lo, q_hi]` to suppress
172	   outliers.
173	2. **Max-abs scale** to `[−1, 1]`: `x' = x / s` with `s = max|x|`.
174	3. **μ-law compand**: `F(x') = sgn(x') · ln(1 + μ|x'|) / ln(1 + μ)`.
175	4. **Uniform bin** the compressed values into `V` bins → integer token IDs.
176	
177	The reference config Cho 2026 ships
178	([`../../external/Cho2026_Tokenizer/models/tokenizer/mu_transform/config.yml`](../../external/Cho2026_Tokenizer/models/tokenizer/mu_transform/config.yml))
179	is:
180	
181	```yaml
182	mu: 255
183	n_tokens: 256
184	normalization: max_abs
185	```
186	
187	There are **no learned weights**, but two parameters are dataset-dependent
188	and must be fit on a THINGS-MEG training split, frozen, and reused at
189	inference:
190	
191	- `s = max|x|`: the per-channel (recommended) or global max-abs scaler.
192	- `q_lo, q_hi`: clip thresholds (defaults around the 0.5 / 99.5 percentiles).
193	
194	`μ` and `V` are hyperparameters, not fit from data — start with μ=255,
195	V=256.
196	
197	Concretely: there is no `.train()` loop. There is a `fit(train_dataset) →
198	calibration.json` step that produces a small JSON sidecar with
199	`{s_per_channel, q_lo, q_hi, mu, V, channel_mode}`. At inference we load
200	that JSON and apply the stateless transform. Treat the calibration JSON as
201	a versioned tokenizer checkpoint.
202	
203	**Phase 1 status (shipped):** calibrated on THINGS-MEG train split with
204	μ=255, V=256, per-channel clip `[0.5, 99.5]` percentiles. Run slug:
205	`V256_mu255_clip0.5-99.5_per_channel_s0`. Eval via
206	`modal_meg_eval.py --tokenizer mu_transform` (see §9).
207	
208	> Verified §3 fact that matters here: all 271 channels are `mag`, so the
209	> grad-vs-mag amplitude disparity problem does not apply. Per-channel `s`
210	> is still the safer default (inter-sensor amplitude variance is real even
211	> within one channel type), but the bin-saturation risk is much lower than
212	> it would be on a mixed-sensor dataset.
213	
214	### Phase 2 — Cho 2026 learnable tokenizer (actual gradient training)
215	
216	The Cho 2026 learnable tokenizer (paper §3.2) is an **autoencoder**, not a
217	VQ-VAE: encoder is GRU → Dense → LayerNorm producing per-time-step logits
218	over a vocab of size `V`; decoder is a bank of `V` 1-D conv kernels (causal
219	or non-causal) summed with learned per-token gains. Trained end-to-end with
220	MSE + an annealed soft-to-hard argmax relaxation, then unused tokens are
221	pruned ("token refactorization").
222	
223	Reference configs they ship
224	(`../../external/Cho2026_Tokenizer/models/tokenizer/{causal,noncausal}/config.yml`):
225	
226	```yaml
227	sequence_length: 200
228	n_channels: 52         # NB: 52 source-parcellated channels, NOT sensor
229	n_tokens: 128
230	token_dim: 10
231	rnn_n_units: 128
232	token_kernel_padding: causal  # or `same` for non-causal
233	training: { lr: 1e-5, batch: 32, epochs: 40, anneal_stages: 40 }
234	```
235	
236	Three things the configs tell us, in light of the verified data spec:
237	
238	1. **They trained on source-reconstructed data (52 parcels), not sensor
239	   space.** Our THINGS-MEG is **271-sensor**. The tokenizer is applied
240	   per-channel and the GRU has no channel-conditioning, so weights *can*
241	   be reused on sensor data, but the *amplitude distribution* of source
242	   parcels (after `osl_dynamics.standardize()`) is not identical to sensor
243	   amplitudes. This is the symmetric MEG version of the LABRAM/THINGS
244	   distribution mismatch in the parent doc.
245	2. **Their sequence_length = 200 samples ≈ 0.8 s.** Our trials are 281
246	   samples (1.4 s). Either reduce our windows to 200 samples (matches their
247	   training distribution → reuse pretrained weights cleanly) or retrain
248	   with `sequence_length = 281`. Strong prior: **window to 200** for 2a/2b
249	   to keep the pretrained encoder in-distribution; only change
250	   `sequence_length` for 2c.
251	3. **Their LR (1e-5) is very small.** Tells us finetuning is intended to be
252	   conservative — they expect the loss landscape to be flat around the
253	   pretrained optimum. Start with that LR and only increase if the loss is
254	   not moving after 1 epoch.
255	
256	"Finetuning on our dataset" then has three concrete recipes:
257	
258	- **2a.** Load pretrained weights, apply on THINGS-MEG **as-is** (zero
259	  finetune). Establishes the floor for what their pretrained tokenizer
260	  buys us on distribution-shifted data.
261	- **2b.** Load pretrained weights, continue MSE training on THINGS-MEG
262	  (lr=1e-5, anneal κ already at 0 → hard tokens — or restart anneal).
263	  This is the "actual finetune."
264	- **2c.** Take their architecture, train from scratch on THINGS-MEG. This
265	  is needed if (a) pretrained weights fail the §5 harness even after 2b,
266	  (b) we want to change `sequence_length` or `n_tokens`, or (c) the
267	  source-vs-sensor mismatch turns out to dominate. With ~108k trials
268	  pooled across P1–P4 (see §3 — much more than the parent doc estimated),
269	  from-scratch is more viable than first assumed.
270	
271	Run **2a → 2b → 2c** in order; stop as soon as the harness numbers are
272	acceptable. Do not skip 2a — the gap between 2a and 2b is the answer to
273	"how much did pretraining buy us."
274	
275	> System-level note: their pretraining data is **resting-state** MEG;
276	> THINGS-MEG is **event-related** (stimulus-locked). Expect the pretrained
277	> kernels to capture continuous oscillatory structure well and to
278	> underweight evoked transients. Reconstruction MSE may look fine while
279	> the §5.3 linear probe quietly tanks — watch that metric closely after
280	> each of 2a/2b/2c.

467	## 8. Evaluation contract
468	
469	Identical to the parent §5. The same `test_tokenizer.py` harness must pass
470	for each of the three MEG tokenizers before it is wired into 4M:
471	
472	1. Reconstruction fidelity (time MSE + per-channel Pearson + **spectral
473	   MSE**). Spectral MSE is the diagnostic metric for MEG — band power
474	   (especially α/β) is what carries task signal. PSD range: 1–100 Hz
475	   (Nyquist for 200 Hz data is 100 Hz).
476	2. Codebook utilization (perplexity, dead-code fraction). The μ-transform's
477	   dead-code fraction is the metric to watch; Cho 2026 prunes dead codes
478	   ("token refactorization") so its effective `V*` can be reported
479	   alongside nominal `V`.
480	3. Linear probe on THINGS labels. With 22,449 image-level codes / 1854
481	   concepts / ~27 superordinate categories, use the THINGS
482	   **superordinate** categories as the primary probe target, 1854-way
483	   concept ID as a secondary, image ID never (too noisy at 27k trials per
484	   subject). The `image_id → concept_id → superordinate` mapping comes
485	   from THINGS metadata, not the .fif files — add it under
486	   `meg/data.py`.
487	4. Token-sequence statistics (unigram entropy, bigram conditional entropy,
488	   run-length). The μ-transform is at particular risk of degenerate run
489	   lengths on slow drifts — watch this.
